I The house

Walk in, and it is 1928 again.

Sir Victor Sassoon opened these rooms to photograph the maharajas, industrialists and film stars of a young, electric Bombay, and named the studio for Lady Hamilton, the most photographed woman of her age.

The chalk-green parlour still wears its original panelling. The chequered stone floor has held the feet of Ambedkar, JRD Tata, Madhubala and the Dalai Lama. Portraits of them all watch you from the walls.

II The instruments

The elders of the house, still working.

The kit has grown with the decades, but two legacy instruments still hold court on the studio floor, and they set the tempo of every sitting here: compose, wait, one exposure.

“

A portrait cannot be hurried. I take my time with every face, until its true essence shows itself.

The Kodak

On duty since 1928

The original box plate camera Sassoon installed on opening day, still standing on the studio floor, still in working order, still making portraits the slow way.

III The archive
0
Negatives, and counting

Glass plates and celluloid going back to opening day: royalty and refugees, wedding parties and passport hopefuls, all of them Bombay. It is one of the most significant photographic collections in India.

The British Library’s Endangered Archives Programme is now helping digitise the earliest plates, so a century of faces can be found again by the families who once sat here.
